Shock Strength Detection

In high-speed flow, the existence of shock waves most often entails either drag increase or  efficiency losses. A major cause of performance degradation is the interaction of the shock  with a boundary layer. These equations are highly dependent on the flow Mach number and hence require near-precise values of the Mach number throughout the flow. This necessitates the need of a way to find the Mach number along the flow with the knowledge of only the boundary conditions. Realising that shocks are the primary source of change in Mach number.  The project was aimed at developing a set of custom equations for detection shock strength & location in a flow. Shockwaves were taken in a shock tube and modelled in MATLAB using 1-dimensional CFD techniques. The Lax-Friedrichs mathematical scheme was used and the obtained computational results were validated against data from theoretical calculations using the equations of compressible flows and Normal & Oblique shock relations. The equations were further modified until reasonable accuracy was obtained for both stationary & moving shocks.   


Scramjet Engine Analysis

The aim of the project was to first understand the various aerodynamic processes involved in the Hypersonic regime followed by performing thermodynamic analysis of a Scramjet Engine, an air-breathing engine capable of producing thrust in that regime. The project began with a study of different types of engines like Turbojet, Ramjet, etc and their performance comparisons. Followed by a study of the rocket equation and compressible flow dynamics. The thermodynamic analysis of a real Scramjet inlet, combustor & outlet were performed where the friction was modelled using Fanno Flow relations while heat addition using Rayleigh flow relations. The dissociation of gas & its effects were also studied at hypersonic speeds. AERODYNAMIC STUDY OF HL-20 DESIGN MODIFICATION

I was a part of the Flying-Wing Lifting-Body team as a research intern at the Society of Space Education & Research Development (SSERD), we started our journey with a head full of questions and the will to contribute. We had the room to think, resources to use and a team of 24 to work with.

The goal was to carry out in-depth research and study quotients that could fetch a quickly accessible database for a multitude of space vehicles. This was followed by narrowing down to a single vehicle and coming up with a conceptualised design iteration that could fulfil analysis criteria and ultimately prove to be efficient over the conventional design. The aim of this Internship, apart from familiarizing with Flying Wing and Lifting Body Designs, was to develop and propose a design optimization idea, which turned out to be in the form of retractable wing configuration over the conventional HL-20 design. The wing extension is needed to provide a larger surface area during re-entry thereby increasing the lift and reducing the ballistic coefficient of the spacecraft.

I contributed in the extensive literature review as well as providing theoretic insights about the shock-boundary layer interactions. Together, we analysed different simulation results and brainstormed on various ideas of improving the aerodynamic parameters of the vehicle, finally reaching the conclusion of adding morphed wings which produced positive results.
